An Introduction to Interim Analysis

An example of RCT

  • Consider a randomized controlled trial with a continuous endpoint
  • The goal is to show that the treatment is superior than the control

Interim analysis

  • An interim analysis is the evaluation of data from a clinical trial before the trial is completed
  • The purposes of an interim analysis include
    • to ensure the safety of the trial participants
    • to check the assumptions made at the design stage
    • to determine if the trial should be stopped due to clear beneficial or harmful effects of the treatment
    • to assess if the trial is likely to reach its objectives
  • Benefits
    • Participants are not exposed to ineffective or unsafe treatments (ethical)
    • Sample size, time and cost are not wasted (economic)
  • Questions
    • When will the interim analyses occur?
    • What data will be used?
    • What statistical methods will be applied?
    • What are the stopping criteria?

Two concepts related to IA

  • Information fraction
    • Defined as the fraction of total information expected at the scheduled end of the trial
    • Roughly, \[\mbox{Information Fraction} = {n \mbox{ at the interim analysis} \over N \mbox{ at the final analysis}}\]
    • For survival analysis, \(n\) and \(N\) are the number of events instead of subjects
  • Maturity
    • No uniformly accepted definition of what maturity means
    • Example
      • Maturity is defined as the proportion of patients that had experienced an event
    • Maturity matters more for the estimation of the survival function

The RCT example

Alpha Spending Function

Definition

  • Any function can be an alpha spending function if the function satisfies
    • \(\alpha(0)=0\) (value equals \(0\) at the beginning of the study)
    • \(\alpha(1)=\alpha\) (value equals \(\alpha\) at the final analysis)
    • Non-decreasing
  • A more flexible group sequential procedure that does not require the total number nor the exact time of the interim analyses to be specified in advance

Alpha spending function examples

  • Pocock-type spending function \[\alpha(t) = \alpha \log \{1 + (e-1)t\} \]
  • OF-type spending function \[\alpha(t) = 2 \{1 - \Phi(z_{\alpha/2}/\sqrt{t})\}\]

An illustration

  • Consider \(\alpha(t) = \alpha t\) with \(\alpha = 0.025\)
  • Suppose the first IA occurs at \(t = 0.2\)
    • The \(\alpha\) to be spent is \(0.2 \times 0.025 = 0.005\)
    • The corresponding critical value \(c_1 = 2.576\), such that \(P(Z_1 > c_1) = 0.005\)
    • Nominal \(\alpha\) at the first IA is \(1 - \Phi(2.576) = 0.005\)
  • Suppose rejection does not happen, and the next IA occurs at \(t=0.5\)
    • The \(\alpha\) that can be spent is \(0.5 \times 0.025 = 0.0125\)
    • The corresponding critical value \(c_2 = 2.377\), such that \(P(Z_1 > 2.576 \cup Z_2 > c_2) = 0.0125\)
    • Nominal \(\alpha\) at the second IA is \(1 - \Phi(2.377) = 0.009\)
  • Suppose reject still does not happen, at the final analysis \(t=1\)
    • The \(\alpha\) that can be spent is \(0.5 \times 0.025 = 0.025\)
    • The corresponding critical value \(c_3 = 2.141\), such that \(P(Z_1 > 2.576 \cup Z_2 > 2.377 \cup Z_3 > c_3) = 0.025\)
    • Nominal \(\alpha\) at the FA is \(1 - \Phi(2.141) = 0.016\)

Primary and secondary endpoint

  • Primary
    • Disease free survival (DFS)
  • Secondary
    • DFS rate at 2, 3 and 5 years
    • Overall Survival (OS)
    • OS rate at 5 years

Interim analysis plan (i)

  • The primary analysis was planned to be conducted when approximately 247 DFS events were observed in the stage II/IIIA population
  • After a planned meeting in 2019 to assess futility, but not superiority, when at least 83 disease recurrence events or deaths had occurred in patients with stage II to IIIA disease, the committee requested assessment of efficacy data at the next scheduled meeting for safety (April 2020)
  • On the basis of review of these data, the committee recommended that the trial be unblinded at a trial level early to complete primary reporting

Potential Limitations

Overestimation of the effect size

  • A trial terminated early for benefit will tend to overestimate the treatment effect
  • Extreme p-values are more likely obtained when there is a random “high” treatment effect
  • Statistical methods exist for adjusting the overestimation bias
    • Not commonly applied in practice
  • It is important to understand the overestimation issue in decision making
  • Zhang, Jenny J., et al. Overestimation of the Effect Size in Group Sequential Trials. Clinical Cancer Research 18.18 (2012)

Clinical relevance (i)

  • Oncologic Drugs Advisory Committee (ODAC) Meeting, February 9, 2021. BLA 125514 Supplement-089
  • KEYNOTE-522
    • Pembrolizumab vs placebo in combination with chemotherapy in the neoadjuvant setting and as monotherapy in the adjuvant setting in 1174 patients with high-risk, early-stage TNBC
    • The co-primary endpoints are pathologic complete response (pCR) rate and event-free survival (EFS)
  • pCR at IA1
    • Pembrolizumab: \(64.8 (59.9, 69.5)\), \(n = 401\)
    • Placebo: \(51.2 (44.1, 58.3)\), \(n=201\)
    • p-value \(0.00055\)

Clinical relevance (ii)

  • The FDA’s position
    • FDA does not agree that the small pCR rate difference between treatment arms is clinically meaningful
    • Interim analyses may overestimate the treatment effect, particularly when the number of events is small
    • The data are not sufficiently mature for FDA to consider these interim EFS results as a reliable estimate of the EFS treatment effect
    • While FDA agrees that pCR was statistically significant at IA1, FDA considers pCR at IA3 to be the most accurate estimate of the pCR difference between treatment arms since this estimate includes all patients who were randomized
